## Copy Sections

### Headline
Original: "Improve your traffic and rankings with an SEOmoz PRO Membership!"
Winning: "When eBay, Disney, and Marriott need SEO help, here's what they do..."

The original headline rushed into a sale. The winning headline generated curiosity and social proof simultaneously. It doesn't sell anything; it makes visitors stop and pay attention.

### Subheadline
Not documented as a separate element. The page flowed from the curiosity headline into a narrative explaining what Fortune 500 companies rely on.

### Hero Body
The winning page matched the CEO's 5-minute in-person sales pitch. The original was a ~1 minute summary. Key insight: they recorded Rand Fishkin selling the product face-to-face at conferences, then compared what he said to what was on the website. The winning page included everything Rand said live that was missing from the site.

The page was 6x longer than the control.

### Primary CTA
$1 trial for full-featured PRO membership (normally $79/month). Replaced previous free trial positioning.

### CTA Context
From Phase II email copy:

"Howdy SEOmozzers, In yesterday's email I made a bit of a mistake...and today I received nearly 500 'feedback' emails as a result! Here's what I didn't make clear: the $1 offer is absolutely no obligation. You get a whole month of SEOmoz PRO, which normally costs $79, for just $1. You don't have to stay signed up for a whole year, you don't even have to stay after the first month. You can sign up, pay one dollar and then leave after 29 days. It'll just cost you a dollar, no more. So, yes, this really is the best offer we've ever made. Why are we making it?"

This follow-up email was triggered by ~500 people responding to the first email saying the $1 offer "sounded like a catch." The email flattened the objection and produced another subscription boost.

### Key Benefits
- Feature comparison chart showing what's included at each membership tier (customers didn't know what tools came with paid vs. free)
- Q&A service with examples showing depth of expert responses (paid customers loved this feature but the site didn't mention it)
- Keyword research, site optimization, link building tools
- The chart allowed hover-over for more detail on each feature

### Social Proof
- Fortune 500 client logos (eBay, Disney, Marriott) displayed prominently. Moz had taken these for granted; they were "ho-hum" about having major clients.
- Long section of testimonials from professional SEO specialists. Peer endorsements from other professional SEOs were even more influential than brand-name logos.
- Video of CEO Rand Fishkin explaining the service (recorded from his live pitch)

### Secondary Sections

**Research methodology that drove the copy:**
They didn't guess what to write. They asked:
- Paying members: "What convinced you to sign up? How would you describe the service to a friend?"
- Free trial members: "What would make you sign up? Which tools do you like most/least?"
- Canceled members: "Why did you cancel? What one thing would bring you back?"
- Recorded the CEO's face-to-face pitch and compared it to the website

**Phase III (retention / lifetime value):**
Quick-start email series: "Get the Most from PRO" tutorial. 10 weekly tips for quick wins within first 30 days. Goal was to get new $1 trial users to experience value fast enough to convert to full-price.

"Hello [Name]! Welcome to your first email in our 'Get the Most from PRO' tutorial series! You'll receive weekly tips and tricks to help you get the most out of SEOmoz PRO and increase your rankings."

**Key principles from the case study:**
1. "You cannot have a page that's too long, only one that's too boring."
2. "Be bold in your tests but conservative in your exposure." The $1 offer was shown only to existing subscribers, not advertised publicly.
3. "Separate yourself from competitors by offering something of value right away."
4. Recommendations can never be better than your facts. Begin by gathering substantial visitor intelligence.
