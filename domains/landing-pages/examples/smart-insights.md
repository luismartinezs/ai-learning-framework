## Copy Sections

### Headline
Not quoted verbatim. The winning page opened with empathy about digital marketing challenges. The goal was to "enter the conversation already going on in their heads" and get prospects nodding along in agreement.

### Subheadline
Not documented separately. The structure: empathy > expert positioning > comprehensive benefits > price anchor > pricing.

### Hero Body
The winning variant added a full sales letter before the pricing page. The original funnel revealed pricing too soon. Newcomers didn't appreciate the depth of resources being offered at the point of decision.

The new page was long. Key copy approach documented in case study:

1. **Entered the prospect's mindset:** Copy designed to get prospects nodding in agreement. "We felt their pain." Acknowledged that digital marketing is fundamentally hard.

2. **Price anchoring:** Positioned Smart Insights as the online equivalent of getting a personal consultation from the world's best digital marketing experts. Put a value on that consulting, then compared the subscription price to it. "Compare the price of a Smart Insights subscription to the cost of having Dave and the team visit you in your office. Suddenly, it transpires that the price is extremely low."

The key framing: "The first rule of Conversion Club: don't pick fights you can't win. If there are free alternatives to your service, make sure you're not competing with them on price." Instead of competing with free marketing blogs, they competed against the cost of personal consulting.

3. **Comprehensive benefit listing:** Members get a lot but the site didn't communicate it. When customers were asked what they liked best, many different answers came back. The winning page listed everything you get as a member and how each item helps.

### Secondary Sections

**Form optimization (91% increase in annual memberships):**
Research revealed three friction points on the signup form:
1. Many prospects joined to solve an urgent problem, so they needed reassurance of instant access to resources
2. Key objections about value lingered through to the form, so prospects needed value reminders even at checkout
3. Many form fields were necessary (Smart Insights needed customer data for personalization) but each was a friction point

Solution: New headline and sidebar promoting identified appeals. Added proof elements. Split the form into two parts. Moved non-essential fields into a post-signup "configure your Smart Insights profile" wizard. This turned an annoying pre-sale form into an appealing onboarding experience.

"Form fields that were annoying in a pre-sale form can be evidently valuable when presented in the onboarding stage."

**Navigation optimization (75% increase in memberships):**
Used tree testing to analyze routes prospects took through navigation. Discovered a group of visitors for whom the information architecture didn't match their mental model.

Original navigation was based on industry standard. Winning navigation used visitors' problems as the starting point, literally asking them to define their problems. Also highlighted site search (visitors who used search were more likely to convert).

"It was a bold decision to test something different. Thanks to Smart Insights' willingness to fly in the face of convention, memberships increased by 75%."

**Research methodology:**
- Became the customer (lived the entire customer journey, not just walked through the funnel)
- Reviewed existing feedback from customer service
- Interviewed real visitors recruited via popup
- User-testing with qualified prospects going through the funnel
- Tree testing for navigation analysis
- Analytics, heatmaps, session recordings
- Designed surveys to collect both data and testimonials simultaneously

**Key principles:**
1. "The research we do before we lay a finger on a website is the most critical activity we undertake on any project."
2. Most businesses skip "Why aren't visitors converting?" and jump to "What should I do about it?" This usually ends in failure.
3. Fresh eyes matter. You can empathize with new visitors in a way that's impossible for someone who's worked at the company for years.
