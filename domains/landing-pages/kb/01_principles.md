# Landing Page Copy Principles

## The Visitor Awareness Spectrum (Eugene Schwartz)

Every visitor arrives at a page in one of five awareness states. The copy must match the state or it fails, regardless of quality.

**Unaware** — The visitor does not know they have a problem. Copy must lead with the world they recognize, not the solution. Open with a scene, a feeling, or a provocation. Never mention the product until the problem is felt.

**Problem Aware** — The visitor knows the pain but not that solutions exist. Copy names the problem with precision. The more accurately you describe their situation, the more they trust you understand it. Do not pitch yet. Validate first.

**Solution Aware** — The visitor knows solutions exist but hasn't chosen one. Copy must differentiate. What makes this approach different from what they've already considered? The mechanism matters here: HOW you solve it, not just THAT you solve it. Generic claims ("we help you grow") get mentally filed with every other option they've seen.

**Product Aware** — The visitor knows your product specifically but hasn't committed. Copy must resolve the remaining objection. Price? Trust? Effort? Risk? Identify the specific hesitation and address it directly. Social proof, risk reversal, and specificity do the heaviest lifting at this stage.

**Most Aware** — The visitor is ready to buy and just needs the right offer or nudge. Copy should be minimal. Get out of the way. Clear CTA, strong offer, zero friction.

The single most common copy failure is writing product-aware copy for problem-aware visitors. The second most common is writing solution-aware copy for unaware visitors. Match the level.


## Job-to-Be-Done Framing

Copy sells the outcome, not the feature. The visitor does not care what the product does. They care what changes in their life after they use it.

A feature is "heatmap analytics." The outcome is "stop guessing which parts of your page visitors actually look at." The feature is "AI-powered writing assistant." The outcome is "publish blog posts in 20 minutes instead of 3 hours."

Every sentence on the page should pass this test: does this describe something the visitor experiences, or something the product contains? If it describes what the product contains, rewrite it as what the visitor experiences.

The job-to-be-done is never the product category. Nobody's job is "get a CRM." Their job is "stop losing track of conversations with prospects." Nobody's job is "use project management software." Their job is "know what everyone on the team is working on without asking."


## The Specificity Principle

Vague claims reduce trust. Specific claims build it. This is not a style preference. It is a trust mechanism.

"We help businesses grow" is meaningless. It triggers no mental image. It could appear on any website in any industry.

"447 e-commerce stores increased checkout completion by an average of 23% within 60 days" is specific. It creates a mental image. It implies measurement. It implies a track record.

Specificity works because it is costly to fake. Anyone can claim "we're the best." Only someone with real data can claim a specific number. The visitor's brain registers this asymmetry even without consciously analyzing it.

Apply specificity to:
- Numbers: "thousands of customers" vs. "11,437 active teams"
- Timeframes: "get results fast" vs. "see your first report in under 4 minutes"
- Mechanisms: "advanced technology" vs. "tracks mouse movement across your page and renders it as a visual heatmap"
- Outcomes: "improve your marketing" vs. "find the exact paragraph where 67% of your readers stop scrolling"


## The Mechanism Principle

Visitors believe HOW more than WHAT. "We'll double your conversions" is a claim. "We record every visitor session as a video replay so you can watch exactly where they hesitate, rage-click, or abandon the page" is a mechanism.

Mechanisms build belief because they make the outcome feel inevitable rather than promised. When a visitor understands the mechanism, they can reason for themselves that it would work. They no longer need to trust your claim. They trust their own judgment.

A claim without a mechanism is an assertion. A claim with a mechanism is an argument.

Every landing page should answer: "How does this actually work?" in concrete, visual terms the visitor can picture. Not technical architecture. Not buzzwords. The specific chain of cause and effect from "I sign up" to "my problem is solved."


## The CTA Commitment Ladder

The call to action should ask for the smallest commitment the page has earned. Nothing more.

A page that explains only what the product category is has earned: "See how it works" (a click).
A page that demonstrates clear value has earned: "Start free trial" (an email).
A page that resolves trust objections has earned: "Start free trial" (email + card).
A page that makes the visitor feel they'd be foolish not to buy has earned: "Buy now."

Mismatched CTAs destroy conversion. "Schedule a demo" on a page that hasn't explained what the product does. "Buy now" when the visitor has seen nothing but a headline. "Get started for free" when the next page asks for a credit card.

The CTA text itself matters. "Get started" is empty. Started with what? "See your heatmap in 60 seconds" tells the visitor exactly what happens next and how long it takes. Specificity in CTAs reduces anxiety because it eliminates the unknown.


## Anti-Patterns

These phrases and patterns are signals of generic, low-converting copy. They appear on thousands of landing pages. They convert poorly because they communicate nothing specific.

**Empty CTAs:**
- "Get started" / "Ready to get started?"
- "Learn more"
- "See what we can do"
- "Request a demo" (when the page hasn't justified why the visitor should spend 30 minutes on a call)

**Hollow claims:**
- "Powerful and flexible"
- "All-in-one solution"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Cutting-edge technology"
- "Trusted by thousands"
- "Simple yet powerful"
- "Everything you need"

**Failure modes:**
- Writing about the company instead of the visitor. "We're passionate about..." The visitor does not care about your passion. They care about their problem.
- Leading with features instead of outcomes. Feature lists belong below the fold, after the visitor understands what changes for them.
- Social proof without specificity. "Trusted by leading companies" means nothing. "Used by eBay, Disney, and Marriott to optimize their search rankings" means something.
- Risk reversal buried or absent. If there's a free trial, money-back guarantee, or no-commitment offer, it should be visible near every CTA, not hidden in the footer.
- Assuming the visitor knows what the product category means. "The leading CDP" assumes the visitor knows what a CDP is. Most don't.
