## Product Description

Moz is a subscription-based SEO software platform and professional community. The toolset includes keyword research, site crawl auditing, link analysis, and rank tracking. Beyond software, the membership includes a Q&A service where subscribers submit their specific SEO questions and receive personalized analysis from in-house experts. The platform operates on a freemium model with a limited free tier and a paid PRO tier that unlocks the full toolset and expert access.

## Target Audience

SEO practitioners and digital marketing professionals. This includes in-house marketers at mid-to-large companies, agency SEO teams, and independent consultants. They already understand what SEO is and why it matters. Many arrive as existing free-tier users who have used some tools but haven't committed to paid. A secondary segment is newcomers referred by industry peers or content marketing.

## Primary Problem Solved

SEO professionals need reliable data and expert guidance to make ranking decisions, but assembling that from disparate free tools and forums is slow and unreliable. The alternative is hiring expensive consultants or relying on guesswork. The core frustration is spending time gathering and cross-referencing SEO data instead of acting on it.

## Key Mechanism

Bundles multiple SEO data tools (keyword, link, crawl, rank) into a single platform, then layers on-demand expert consultation on top. The Q&A service is the differentiator: subscribers get personalized analysis of their specific sites, not generic advice. This combines tool access with human expertise in a single subscription.

## Constraints

- Freemium model: free tier exists, so paid tier must justify the delta
- Monthly subscription pricing ($79/month at the time of the case study)
- The founder (Rand Fishkin) is a well-known industry personality, which is a trust asset
- Enterprise clients (Fortune 500) use the platform, which is a credibility asset but not widely known to prospects
- The expert Q&A feature is beloved by paid members but poorly understood by prospects
