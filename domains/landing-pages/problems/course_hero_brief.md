## Product Description

Course Hero is a crowdsourced study platform for college students. Users upload and access course-specific study materials including notes, flashcards, practice problems, and homework solutions. The platform also provides access to expert tutors for on-demand help. It operates as a two-sided marketplace: students contribute study documents (supply side) and access the library and tutors (demand side). The model is freemium, with a paid tier for full access to the resource library.

## Target Audience

US college students actively looking for study help, homework assistance, or exam preparation materials. They typically arrive via search (looking for specific course materials) or peer referral. They are under time pressure (studying for exams, completing assignments) and price-sensitive (student budgets). There are two conversion goals: getting students to pay for full access, and getting students to upload their own study materials to grow the library.

## Primary Problem Solved

College students need course-specific study resources (notes from their exact class, solutions to their actual homework problems) but can't find them through general search or textbooks. Study groups are inconsistent. Office hours are limited. Tutors are expensive. The gap is between needing targeted, course-specific help and having access to it on demand at an affordable price.

## Key Mechanism

Crowdsourcing from a community of over 10 million students and educators. Because users upload materials from their own courses, the library contains course-specific resources that no publisher or tutor service could create at scale. The network effect means the library gets more valuable as more students join and contribute. Expert tutors supplement the crowdsourced library for questions that need human explanation.

## Experiment Scope

Primary conversion goal for this experiment: paid subscription signup.
Document upload optimization is a secondary goal, ignore for copy generation.

## Constraints

- Freemium model: free account creation, paid tier for full access (but prospects don't know it's free to create an account)
- Two-sided platform: must optimize both document uploads (supply) and paid subscriptions (demand) without conflating the two goals
- Facebook signup option exists but creates friction (students don't want to link social media to a study tool)
- Many visitors land on specific study resource pages from Google, not the homepage, so they need product education in context
- Student budget sensitivity: any paid tier competes against "free" alternatives
- Platform network effects mean growth speed matters strategically
