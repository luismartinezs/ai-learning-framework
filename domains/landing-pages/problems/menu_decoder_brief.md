## Product Description
Menu Decoder is a PWA that helps tourists with dietary restrictions navigate foreign-language menus. Users photograph a menu with their phone, and AI (Google Gemini 2.0 Flash) performs OCR, translates every dish to English, and flags potential dietary violations using a red/yellow/green system. It also generates a "Staff Card," a translated message listing all your restrictions that you show to restaurant staff in their language.

## Target Audience
Tourists and travelers with dietary restrictions (allergies, vegan, vegetarian, religious dietary laws) visiting countries where they cannot read the local menu language. They are already abroad or actively planning a trip. They are health-conscious or medically constrained. They have likely already tried Google Translate on menus and found it unreliable for identifying hidden ingredients. They arrive from travel forums, social media, or search with no prior awareness of the product.

## The Moment of Need
You are sitting in a restaurant in Bangkok. The menu is entirely in Thai script. You have a peanut allergy. The waiter does not speak English. You are staring at a page of characters you cannot read, afraid to order because the wrong dish could mean a hospital visit in a foreign country. Google Translate gives you literal word translations but tells you nothing about whether the curry paste contains peanuts.

## Primary Problem Solved
Eliminates the fear and guesswork of ordering food abroad when you have dietary restrictions and cannot read the menu.

## Key Mechanism
AI-powered vision analysis reads the menu photo, translates every dish, and cross-references each one against your dietary profile, including traditional ingredients that are not listed on the menu but are commonly used in that cuisine.

## Trust Barriers
- "What if the AI gets it wrong? My allergy is life-threatening." The product handles health-adjacent decisions, which raises the stakes beyond a typical app.
- "I could just use Google Translate for free." Visitors will compare against free tools and wonder what the paid product adds.
- "I'm only traveling for a week. Is it worth paying for a tool I'll use for a few days?" Short trip duration makes the value proposition feel expensive per-use.
